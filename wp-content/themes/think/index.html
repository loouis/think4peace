THINK is a bespoke programme aimed at young people aged 14–19 that equips them to THINK critically and confidently about their place in society, and in the process develops their skills, knowledge and understanding around core values and beliefs, allowing them to play a positive role in managing conflict.

Who we are. Watch the above video to find out about who we are.

THINK cultivates curiosity, challenges attitudes, breaks habits and increases resilience to the threat of radicalisation leading to violent extremism by encouraging young people to act rather than react.

All courses take place at the Tim Parry Jonathan Ball Foundation for Peace in Warrington, which was set up by Tim’s parents, supported by Jonathan’s, after both children were killed in the IRA bomb that exploded in the centre of Warrington in 1993.

The Challenge

Re-THINK

At the moment three schools are involved with the THINK project, but given the impact it’s having we want to be in a position to roll this out across many more schools. In order to achieve that, we require extra sponsorship and funding.

Young people who have been excluded from school, or have a difficult home life, are more susceptible than others to the lure of gangs, or the perceived ‘glamour’ of extremism, whether that be far right groups such as the EDL or jihadi groups operating either here or abroad.

Our challenge is to get these young people to re-THINK the way they behave, encouraging them to become responsive rather than reactive.

Often they are unaware that their beliefs and values directly affect their attitude and behaviour, so with THINK we help them challenge their current beliefs and attitudes that justify violent responses to conflict.

We are fully aware that violent extremist groups use prejudice and discrimination as a way to gain support, so it’s vital that we make young people realise that there is another way, and that they can turn their back on extremism and become positive leaders within their peer group and society as a whole.

  • Methodology

    Think Stages of Delivery

  • One-day introductory session at the school or college which is taking part.

  • Initial three-day residential course at the Tim Parry Jonathan Ball Foundation for Peace Centre.

  • Further three-day residential course at the Tim Parry Jonathan Ball Foundation for Peace Centre.

  • One-day follow-up sessions at the school or college which has taken part.

Introductory Session

We visit the schools and colleges taking part and spend a day with the young people who are going to benefit from THINK, by introducing the programme and explaining what the Foundation does. This acts as a bit of a taster for them, and as they’re in familiar surroundings they feel safe and secure and are more likely to open up to us. The facilitator for the programme chats to each of them personally, and then they take part in a group session.

 
 
THINK recruitment

We work with relevant schools to identify those young people who would benefit most from taking part in THINK. We then have a chat with each of them to explain what THINK entails, so we can work out if there are any specific needs that need addressing to ensure that they get the most out of the programme.

Two Three-day long residential programmes

The first of these carries on the conversations from the introductory stage, but goes on to explore some of the issues already raised and explores them in greater depth. There are never more than 16 young people on any residential course, and there are two facilitators who work with the group.

The Benefits

THINK

Over the course of the three days, those taking part will hear from survivors, experts, and former extremists who share their stories, and will role-play real life situations, putting in place new strategies to manage conflict and to challenge extremism, prejudice and discrimination. Participants learn and practice cognitive life skills by looking at theirs and others’ behaviour, questioning what’s behind it, and trying to find a solution that is constructive rather than destructive.

THINK benefits those who take part in the following ways:

They will be equipped with the necessary skills to argue against negative and violent opinions and will have found ‘their voice’ in order to implement change.

Thinking Skills

They will be able to think critically about themselves, other people, and have the confidence to understand and challenge the consequences of personal, social and political conflict. Their appreciation of other people’s views will be enhanced and their susceptibility to extremist narratives reduced.

Countering Violent Extremism

They will be confident in challenging simplistic narratives, and feel comfortable in expressing their own views in order to put their own point across.

Conflict Resolution

They will have the skills, confidence and capacity to manage and resolve personal, social and political conflicts by non-violent means.

Self-Awareness

They will have the ability to see how beliefs and values directly affect behaviour and attitudes, and will gain in emotional intelligence, giving them a greater understanding how their attitudes and behaviour affects others.

A Network of Leaders

They will become part of a network of graduate Peace Builders and the Foundation’s THINK-tank where they will have support from their peers and the organisation.
